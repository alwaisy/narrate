| SECTION 1: STRATEGY OVERVIEW |
| :---- |

## **The Core Framework**

Everything you publish follows one editorial spine:

| Problem → Solution → Product → Founder This is what separates you from every other indie media account. Everyone else leads with the founder. You lead with the problem. That single choice makes your content more useful to the reader and more credible to the founder being covered. |
| :---- |

## **Your Three Content Pillars**

| Pillar | Name | Core Idea |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Pillar 1** | **Problem-First Product Stories** | Lead with the problem. Show the product. Reveal the founder. Nobody else does this. |
| **Pillar 2** | **The Decisions That Actually Mattered** | Go deep on the forks in the road. Tech stack. Pricing. First customer channel. The real logic behind them. |
| **Pillar 3** | **Building Without a Safety Net** | The honest side. Motivation collapse. Almost quitting. What $1k MRR actually changes. |

**These three pillars are not interchangeable. Each one does a specific job.**

* Pillar 1 acquires new readers through the product and problem angle.

* Pillar 2 converts them into believers because the depth is real.

* Pillar 3 keeps them coming back because they feel seen.

## **Pillar 1: Problem-First Product Stories**

These are your anchor posts. The format is always: open with the problem, build to the product, end with the founder. Keep each section lean.

**Format A: The Full Teardown (Monday post)**

| Structure Hook (1-2 lines on the problem). Why this problem exists. Who suffers from it. What they tried before. The product that answered it. 3 real decisions the founder made. One honest number. One thing that almost killed it. |
| :---- |

**Content ideas in this format:**

* A freelancer in Lagos can’t invoice in USD. Here’s the tool one person built to fix it. ($1.2k MRR, built alone, 9 months)

* Product Hunt analytics are useless for solo founders. Here’s why one builder stopped using them and what they track instead.

* Notion is free. So why did 400 paying customers choose this $9/month Notion alternative? The problem it solves is narrower than you think.

* UX researchers drown in sticky notes after interviews. Here’s the solo-built tool that tags and organizes them in 60 seconds.

* Most CRMs are built for sales teams. This founder built one for consultants who work alone. $340 MRR in month 3\.

* Scheduling calls across time zones is a daily tax. One founder got tired of paying it and built a fix. Here’s what the first 6 months looked like.

* Solo accountants in Pakistan can’t find invoicing software that works in PKR and USD. One engineer left Berlin to fix that.

* Usability testing costs $5k and takes two weeks with a research firm. This designer built a 20-minute alternative for $29/month.

**Format B: The Problem Alone (short post, high engagement)**

| Structure Describe a specific, frustrating problem in 4-6 lines. Ask readers if they’ve felt it. No product mentioned. Use the comments to find founders who built solutions and feature them later. |
| :---- |

**Content ideas:**

* You ship a product. Product Hunt gives you 47 upvotes and 3 sign-ups. Traffic goes to zero the next day. What now?

* You’ve been building for 6 months. You have 0 customers. You don’t know if this is normal or if it’s already dead.

* You find a competitor with worse UX, worse features, and 10x your users. They just raised $2M. How do you not quit?

* Your first review is a 1-star. The person misunderstood what your product does. Do you respond? Pivot? Ignore?

* You price at $29/month. Nothing. Drop to $9/month. One sign-up. You don’t know if it’s the price or the product.

## **Pillar 2: The Decisions That Actually Mattered**

These go deep on one specific choice. Not lessons. Not takeaways. One decision, with the real logic behind it.

**Format A: The Decision Breakdown**

| Structure Name the decision in the first line. What were the two options? Why did they pick one over the other? What happened as a result? Would they do it again? |
| :---- |

**Content ideas:**

* They chose Postgres over MongoDB. Here’s the exact reason. Three years later, here’s whether they regret it.

* They launched free. Then switched to paid at month 4\. What that decision cost them in users and what it gave them back.

* They picked email as their only distribution channel. No Twitter. No LinkedIn. No SEO. Here’s what happened to MRR.

* They didn’t build an onboarding flow for the first 5 months. Here’s the churn number that forced them to finally build it.

* They charged $5/month to start. Then raised to $29/month overnight. Here’s how many customers left and how many stayed.

* They built on Nuxt instead of Next. Why. What it cost them in hiring. Whether they’d change it.

* They said no to a $15k contract that would have required custom features. Here’s the math behind that decision.

* They chose to build in public with 40 followers. Here’s what that looked like for the first 60 days and what it finally started returning.

**Format B: The Stack Post**

| Structure Share the exact tech stack of a solo-built product. Why each tool was chosen. What the monthly cost is. What they’d swap if starting over. |
| :---- |

**Content ideas:**

* This founder runs a $2k MRR product on $47/month in infrastructure. Here’s every tool and why.

* They rebuilt their entire auth system after a security scare. Here’s what they changed and what it cost in dev time.

* Supabase vs. PlanetScale. A solo founder who tried both tells you what actually mattered at the 0-to-$1k MRR stage.

* They added a background job queue at month 5\. Here’s the exact moment they knew they needed it and what they built with.

## **Pillar 3: Building Without a Safety Net**

These are the posts that keep people coming back. Honest, specific, no motivational wrapping paper.

**Format A: The Almost-Quit Moment**

| Structure Describe the specific moment a founder nearly stopped. What was happening in the product. What was happening in their life. What made them keep going. Where they are now. |
| :---- |

**Content ideas:**

* Month 6\. Zero paying customers. $4k left in savings. The Stripe dashboard showing nothing. Here’s what they did next.

* Their first 1-on-1 user call lasted 45 minutes. The user told them the product was confusing. They almost deleted the repo that night.

* A funded competitor launched the same product 3 weeks after them. Here’s how they responded.

* They got featured in a newsletter with 12k subscribers. 3 sign-ups. 0 conversions. Here’s what that taught them about distribution.

* They worked on it every night for 4 months. Their partner asked them to stop. Here’s how they made the decision.

**Format B: The Real Number Post**

| Structure Share a real MRR milestone from a founder (with their permission). Not just the number. The before and after. What changed. What stayed hard. |
| :---- |

**Content ideas:**

* $0 to $1k MRR. How long it took. What finally moved the number. What they wish they’d done 3 months earlier.

* $210 MRR. Strong retention. Zero growth. Here’s what that feels like and what the founder is actually doing about it.

* $3k MRR in 6 months. No audience. No ads. No Product Hunt spike. Here’s the only channel that worked.

* They hit $1k MRR. Then lost 40% of it in one month when a free competitor launched. Here’s what happened after.

**Format C: Personal Post (Your Own Build)**

| Structure Your own experience building Fewwords. Real decisions. Real moments. No polishing. This is the content that builds the deepest trust because you are the ICP. |
| :---- |

**Content ideas:**

* Why I chose to build a summarization tool instead of something with an obvious market.

* The week I had 0 users and considered pivoting. What stopped me.

* What building Fewwords taught me about the difference between a real problem and a personal problem.

* I added SSE and a job queue to Fewwords. Here’s why. Here’s what it cost in dev time vs. what it gave back.

* My first paying user. What they said. What I expected them to say. Why the gap matters.

## **Weekly Publishing Schedule**

This is a 5-touch-per-week schedule. Three LinkedIn posts, one newsletter, one blog post. Manageable alone if you batch everything on the right days.

| Day | Format | Pillar | What You Post |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Monday** | LinkedIn Post | Pillar 1 | Problem-first product teardown. Full editorial format: Problem → Solution → Product → Founder. |
| **Wednesday** | LinkedIn Post | Pillar 2 | Decision deep-dive. One specific choice a solo founder made. Why they made it. What happened. |
| **Friday** | LinkedIn Post | Pillar 3 | Honest moment. Almost quit. Motivation crash. Or a real win with context attached. |
| **Saturday** | Newsletter | All 3 | Weekly issue. Expanded version of the Monday teardown \+ one decision \+ one honest moment. |
| **Sunday** | Blog Post | Rotating | Long-form piece. Keyword-targeted. Built from the week's content but expanded with research. |

## **The Batching System**

Trying to create content daily will kill your output within 3 weeks. Batch instead. One heavy work session per week produces everything.

| The Wednesday Session (3-4 hours) This is your main production block. Find your founder story. Conduct the interview or pull from research. Write the full teardown. Then extract the 3 LinkedIn posts from it. Extract the newsletter main feature. Outline the blog post. Everything for the week comes from this session. |
| :---- |

| The Friday Session (1-2 hours) Write and schedule the 3 LinkedIn posts. Write and schedule the newsletter. Do a first draft of the blog post. Light editing. You’re not perfecting anything here. You’re finishing. |
| :---- |

| The Sunday Session (1 hour) Finalize and publish the blog post. Check comments on the week’s LinkedIn posts. Reply to everyone. Note which post performed best. Use that signal for next week’s content direction. |
| :---- |

## **The Content Repurposing Loop**

Every piece of content feeds the next one. Nothing gets created from scratch more than once.

| Starting Point Founder interview or story LinkedIn Monday post Newsletter main feature Blog post Comments \+ engagement | Becomes → 3 LinkedIn posts \+ 1 newsletter \+ 1 blog post → Newsletter expanded version (Sat) → Blog post with research added (Sun) → Future LinkedIn posts (evergreen quotes, stats) → New founder leads for next week’s story |
| :---- | :---- |

## **Finding Founders to Feature**

This is the sourcing system. Do it proactively. You should always have 3-4 founders in the pipeline ready to be featured.

* Search Indie Hackers every week for posts with real MRR numbers and zero media coverage.

* Monitor r/SaaS and r/indiehackers for founders sharing honest updates, not wins.

* Check Product Hunt for launches that got fewer than 100 upvotes but have paying customers.

* Watch your LinkedIn comments. Founders who engage with your posts are telling you they exist.

* Ask directly at the end of your newsletter. 'Building something and willing to share the real numbers? Reply to this email.'

* Build a simple submission form. Link to it from every LinkedIn bio and newsletter footer.

## **What to Track**

Don’t measure vanity. Measure signals that tell you if the content is working.

| Metric LinkedIn follower growth (weekly) Newsletter open rate Newsletter reply rate Blog organic sessions Inbound story submissions Saves on LinkedIn posts | Why it matters Distribution reach is growing Subject line quality \+ list health Trust level. This is your most important metric. SEO compounding over time Your reputation is working Indicates content worth revisiting and sharing |
| :---- | :---- |

## **The 6-Month Execution Plan**

Month 1 to 2: Build the machine. Publish consistently. Establish your format. Don’t optimize. Just ship.

Month 3 to 4: Compound. Watch which content gets the most saves and shares. Do more of that. Start building the submission pipeline.

Month 5 to 6: Go wider. Your best posts from months 1-4 get republished as blog posts with more research. Your newsletter list grows from your LinkedIn. Founders start DMing you.

| The one rule that overrides everything else Consistency beats quality for the first 6 months. A good post published today beats a perfect post published never. You’re building a track record, not a portfolio. Publish. Every week. Without fail. |
