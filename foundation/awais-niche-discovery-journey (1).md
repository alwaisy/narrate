# Niche Discovery Journey
### Awais — February 2026

---

## Where It Started

The session opened with a clear goal: find a niche specific enough that Awais could own the top 1% of it, build content around it, and eventually monetize through an offer. The constraints were tight. The niche had to have at least 3 levels of specificity, speak to a wealthy or monetizable audience, and be framed uniquely enough that content around it would stand out on its own.

The starting point was Awais's profile: a Vue/Nuxt engineer, solo builder of Fewwords, ex-PakStartups media operator, LinkedIn content creator, and someone who had been quietly watching the indie hacker and startup ecosystem for three years. On the surface, a lot of directions were possible. The first job was to cut through the noise.

---

## What the First Round of Questions Revealed

The early questions were designed to find what Awais was genuinely good at, not just what he had done. His answers pointed in three directions at once: personal branding, product growth, and founder-led marketing. Broad territory.

His actual post list told a sharper story. Mixed in with engineering opinion pieces were founder spotlights, ecosystem takes, and posts about Pakistani tech infrastructure. But he made something clear early: he had already left the Pakistani ecosystem. Low reach, little growth, an audience that wasn't ready for what he was doing. He had moved on and was thinking globally.

That eliminated a whole category of niche ideas before they were even written down.

---

## The Pattern That Emerged

When asked what he saw that other indie hackers missed, Awais gave a specific and confident answer. He talked about UX gaps, mobile responsiveness, wrong tech stack choices, the difference between shipping a generic v1 and shipping something actually differentiated. He had given a 10th-grade student founder a full, serious product plan for a naming tool. He had advised a LinkedIn product team on feature prioritization, pushing them to go vertical instead of horizontal.

He called himself a beginner. The evidence said otherwise.

What he actually was, across all of his answers, was a pattern recognizer. Someone who had spent three years watching products grow from zero, watching founders make the same mistakes, and quietly giving advice that worked. One founder he helped landed their first paying customer. He said he was "so happy" about it. That line mattered more than anything else he said.

At this point the working hypothesis was: product strategy consultant for early-stage indie hackers. A niche built around product audits, UX feedback, and go-to-market advice for solo technical founders.

---

## The First Major Pivot

Ten niche statements were presented based on that hypothesis. Awais pushed back immediately.

Consulting and auditing were never the main goal. They were side hustle territory, something he did informally. His real goal was to become a content creator in the indie hacker industry. Not an advisor. A media voice.

He described something closer to a TechCrunch for indie hackers. Curated Reddit content made consumable. Founder stories brought to the public. Product news and articles. A YouTube channel with weekly founder interviews. A newsletter that featured indie products the way Realistic Optimist features emerging market startups.

The framing shifted entirely. This was no longer about selling consulting hours. It was about building an audience and a media brand.

---

## The Realistic Optimist Reference

Awais pointed to Realistic Optimist as his inspiration model. Once it was described, the connection became clear. Realistic Optimist is a newsletter and magazine for venture capitalists covering startups in emerging markets. Its edge is geography and honesty. It covers places and founders that mainstream tech media ignores, and it tells the truth about how hard building actually is.

Awais wanted to do the same thing, but for a different world. Not VC-backed startups. Solo-built products. Not investors as the audience. Consumers and early founders.

The geographic angle that powers Realistic Optimist would not be his edge. His edge needed to be something else.

---

## Finding the Real Angle

The question of content angle led to the most important exchange of the session.

Awais made an observation about Product Hunt that reframed everything. Product Hunt started as a consumer discovery tool. Somewhere along the way it became founder-facing. Fake upvotes, coordinated launch campaigns, founders talking to founders. The consumer got pushed out. There is no clean place for an everyday person to go and find indie products worth using. App Store and Play Store solved this for native apps by keeping founders at the backend. The web product world has nothing equivalent.

He wants to fix that. Not right now, and not as a product. First as content. Build the audience, then build the platform.

That insight handed him his editorial angle: **problem-first storytelling**.

Instead of leading with the founder the way every other indie media property does, he leads with the problem. Why does this problem exist? Who has it? Then here is the solution someone built. Then the product itself. Then the founder. Problem, solution, product, founder. That ladder is his framework and it is genuinely different from how this space currently works.

---

## The Final Niche Statement

Ten refined niche statements were written around this framework. The clearest version of what was landed on:

> "I build a consumer-first media layer for the indie product world, telling problem-first stories on LinkedIn and newsletter, so great solo-built products find the audience they deserve before the founder runs out of motivation."

The core components: problem-first editorial lens, LinkedIn plus newsletter as the format, solo-built products as the subject matter, and honest discovery as the value. No hype, no fake upvotes, no founder worship. Just real problems, real solutions, real people.

---

## Where Things Ended

The niche was locked. Awais confirmed the content goal was clear and decided to move the conversation to a separate session focused on ICP, the ideal customer or reader profile.

The session ended with a clean summary: problem-first indie product media, built on LinkedIn and newsletter, leading always with the problem before the founder. The angle is honest discovery in a space full of noise. The long-term product vision, a consumer-first indie product discovery platform, sits behind the content strategy as the destination once the audience exists.

The foundation is there. The next step is defining exactly who this is built for.

*Session completed February 2026.*
