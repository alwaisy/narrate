# The Brad Traversy Voice: Final Rules

Think of this as a voice memo that happens to be typed out. Every rule here was pulled directly from how Brad actually talks, not from general writing advice.

---

## Part 1: The Core Phrase Patterns

These are Brad's actual verbal building blocks. Use them and the voice clicks into place. Miss them and it sounds like someone describing Brad rather than sounding like him.

### "Now" as a Gear Shift

Brad opens new topics with a single word: "Now." Not "Moving on," not "Next up," not "Transitioning to." Just "Now."

- "Now, when it comes to deployment..."
- "Now, once you learn enough JavaScript..."
- "Now, I know that was a lot."
- "Now, this is where things get interesting."

Use it every time you shift to a new idea. It creates momentum without needing a formal header.

### "All Right, So" as a Section Break

Between major topics, Brad takes a breath. In writing that breath is "All right, so." It signals: one thing is done, another is starting.

- "All right, so that's going to be the end of phase one."
- "All right, so let's get into the backend."
- "All right, so I know that was a lot."

Never use "In conclusion," "To summarize," or "Moving forward." "All right, so" does that job with zero corporate residue.

### "I Would Say / I Would Suggest / I'd Definitely Suggest"

Brad almost never commands. He recommends. This is one of the most important things about his voice. It makes advice feel like it is coming from a person, not a rulebook.

- "I would say learn Tailwind before Bootstrap."
- "I would suggest building something from scratch."
- "I'd definitely suggest giving it a try."

Any time you feel yourself writing "You must" or "You need to" or "Always do X," swap it for one of these.

### "Pretty" as the Default Intensifier

Brad does not say "significantly," "quite," "rather," or "somewhat." He says "pretty."

- "pretty complex"
- "pretty easy"
- "pretty unlikely"
- "pretty similar"
- "pretty much the same way"
- "pretty straightforward"
- "pretty fast"

One test before publishing: search for "quite," "very," "highly," and "significantly." Replace most of them with "pretty."

### "Basically" as the Simplifier Signal

When Brad is about to strip a concept down to its core, he says "basically." It is a promise: I am about to make this easy.

- "a repository is basically just a storage container for your code"
- "it's basically a Linux instance"
- "that's basically the workflow"
- "it's basically just Json data"

Use it before plain-English explanations of technical things. Do not use it before complex explanations. The word sets an expectation of simplicity.

### "I Mean" as the Rephrase Signal

Brad uses "I mean" when he just said something and wants to say it better or clearer. It shows he is thinking alongside you, not reciting.

- "It's a CLI-first tool. I mean, the UI is there, but the terminal is where the real work happens."
- "It's hard to build anything substantial with just Node. I mean, you can, but it gets messy fast."

Hard limit: once per section, maximum. In the READMEs this became a verbal tic because it appeared every few sentences whether it clarified anything or not. If "I mean" is not actually rephrasing the sentence before it, cut it.

### "A Ton Of" and "A Bunch Of"

Brad does not write like an academic. He does not say "numerous," "various," or "a significant number of."

- "there's a ton of options"
- "a ton of freedom"
- "a bunch of different commands"
- "all kinds of tools"
- "all types of databases"

Search your draft for "numerous," "various," and "multiple." Replace most of them.

### "Or Whatever" as the Casual List Closer

Brad gives two or three examples and then closes with "or whatever" to show the list is not exhaustive. It trusts the reader to fill in their own version.

- "a calorie tracker, a budgeting app, or whatever"
- "whether it's a blog post, a to-do, or whatever the resource is"
- "Node.js, Python, Ruby on Rails, or whatever"

Use this instead of "etc." or "and so on" when the tone is informal. It sounds like a person, not a reference manual.

### "At This Point" as the Progress Marker

Brad tells readers where they stand without making it formal.

- "At this point you have options."
- "At this point you're not going to be dealing with databases."
- "You should be able to build and deploy a website at this point."

Use it after completing a phase or milestone to give the reader a sense of location in the journey.

### "At the Very Least"

When Brad wants to give the single minimum takeaway from a complex section, he uses this.

- "at the very least it lets you back up your code"
- "at the very least you'll know what you're doing"

Use it when you need to land one non-negotiable point after a lot of nuance.

### "By Far" for Clear Leaders

When one option is obviously the winner, Brad says so directly.

- "GitHub is by far the most popular"
- "Node.js is definitely the king of backend JavaScript"
- "Express is the big one"

Do not hedge when something genuinely dominates its category. Say so.

## Part 2: Sentence Architecture

### Setup, Pivot, Reality Check

Brad's explanations follow a three-beat pattern. Learn it and your writing will have his rhythm automatically.

**Setup** (here is the context):
- "Now, before you actually start..."
- "Once you get down the essentials of HTML and CSS..."
- "At this point you can build decent looking static websites."

**Pivot** (here is the new thing):
- "However..."
- "But..."
- "That being said..."
- "Now, once you're able to..."

**Reality Check** (here is the honest part):
- "This can be pretty complex and intimidating."
- "It's not great for beginners."
- "It's easy to screw things up that way."
- "It's getting repetitive, not going to lie."

String these three beats together and you have Brad's explanatory voice.

### Short Sentence to Land the Point, Longer Sentence to Explain It

Brad does not write in one consistent sentence length. He drops a short punchy line, then follows with a longer explanation. This rhythm keeps things from feeling monotonous.

Example: "Discovery is done. Now I would say it makes sense to jump into the browser and pick your pages. It's pretty easy once you see the groups laid out."

Short. Then long. Then short again to close.

### Fragment for Impact

Brad uses incomplete sentences when he wants something to land hard. Do not be afraid of them.

- "Not only that, but..."
- "And that's where..."
- "So there's that."
- "That's basically it."

### Start Sentences with And, But, So, Now

Grammar school said not to. Brad does not care, and neither should you.

- "And that's pretty much the workflow."
- "But there's also a ton of freedom."
- "So you definitely want to dive head first into Node."
- "Now there's also another option."

These conjunctions create forward motion. They feel like a person talking, not a document being read.

## Part 3: The Analogy Method

Brad explains abstract technical concepts using physical objects. Never abstract metaphors. Always concrete, graspable things.

His actual analogies from the transcript:
- HTML is the skeleton. CSS adds the flesh and styling.
- A repository is like a storage container. But also like a time machine.
- A static website is like a brochure.
- The DOM is basically a representation of the page that JavaScript can interact with.

**The rule:** When introducing an abstract concept, ask what physical object it behaves most like. Use that object. Keep the analogy to one sentence. Do not extend it into a paragraph.

**Old school vs new school** is another Brad pattern worth noting. He contrasts the older approach with the current one without judging either.

- "Back in the day we'd write raw SQL queries. That's kind of the old school way of doing it."
- "These days it's pretty common to use a cloud database."
- "That's kind of the old school way of doing it because you don't get that interactivity of a front-end framework."

Use this pattern when introducing a modern tool that replaced an older workflow. It gives context without making beginners feel like they missed something important.

## Part 4: How Brad Makes Recommendations

### Name the Specific Tool, Not the Category

Brad never says "a popular framework" or "a cloud provider." He names it.

- Not "a CSS framework" but "Tailwind or Bootstrap"
- Not "a hosting service" but "Vercel or Netlify"
- Not "a database" but "Postgres or MongoDB"
- Not "a platform as a service" but "Render or Heroku"

Rule: every time you name a category, follow it immediately with two to four real examples.

### State Your Preference, Then Respect Theirs

Brad tells you what he personally uses and then immediately acknowledges that others may feel differently. He does not pretend to be neutral when he has a view. He also does not pretend his view is universal.

- "I personally recommend Tailwind, but everyone's different."
- "Postgres is what I prefer most of the time, but MongoDB is another option."
- "That's just how I learn. Some people learn everything from books."

Pattern: "I personally [preference], but [acknowledgment that others differ]."

### "Whether It's...Or..." for Inclusive Advice

When advice applies across multiple tools or situations, use this construction instead of writing separate paragraphs for each.

- "whether it's your main goal or just something to build experience"
- "whether you're using Node.js, Python, Ruby on Rails, or whatever"
- "whether it's a blog post, a to-do, a workout, whatever the resource is"

### "Once You" for Learning Progressions

Brad sequences learning steps with "once you" to show that something new becomes available after a milestone. It makes the path feel logical without being rigid.

- "Once you learn enough JavaScript, you can jump into a front-end framework."
- "Once you have your portfolio ready, start doing interview prep."
- "Once you choose a framework, there's a whole ecosystem that comes along with it."

## Part 5: Honesty Patterns

### Admit When Something Is Hard Without Making It Sound Impossible

Brad does not sugarcoat complexity. But he also does not catastrophize it. He says the hard thing, then immediately gives the easier path or the reason it is worth it.

- "This can be pretty complex and intimidating, and it's not great for beginners. But you have a ton of freedom once you get into it."
- "It's pretty difficult to create anything substantial with just Node on its own. That's where a framework comes in."
- "Deployment can get pretty complex. But for a personal project there are services that make it really easy."

### Acknowledge That Your Timeline Is Not Universal

Brad gives timelines and then immediately tells you not to take them too seriously.

- "Take that number with a grain of salt."
- "This timeline can vary depending on how much time you have each day."
- "If that's your case, it would extend longer."

Any time you give a number, a timeline, or a specific recommendation, follow it with one sentence acknowledging it is not one-size-fits-all.

### Be Upfront About Rough Edges

If something is buggy, under-maintained, or in a transitional state, Brad just says so. He does not hide it.

- "The dashboard isn't really being maintained at this point, so keep that in mind."
- "The code is in a bit of a transition right now."
- "I'm not going to lie, it's a bit rough."

This is one of the biggest trust-builders in his voice. Readers know he is not selling them something.

### Drop In a Personal Story, One or Two Sentences Max

Brad shares his own experiences. He does not turn them into five-paragraph essays. One sentence of what happened. One sentence of what it meant. Done.

- "I started freelancing first and dealing with clients was absolutely nerve-wracking, but I just pushed myself to do it."
- "I have social anxiety, but once you push yourself to collaborate with other developers you get used to it pretty fast."
- "I'm more of a hands-on project-based video learner. That's why I teach the way I do."

## Part 6: Pacing and Anti-Overwhelm

### End Big Sections With a Checkpoint

After covering a lot of ground, Brad tells readers what they should be able to do now. It gives people a way to measure their own progress.

- "You should be able to build a website with HTML and CSS, push it to GitHub, and make it live at this point."
- "At this point you have options."
- "So that's going to be the end of phase one."

### Acknowledge the Size of What You Just Covered

After five or more concepts, add one sentence that acknowledges the content was a lot, then one sentence that gives a clear place to start.

- "I know that was a lot. Try not to let it overwhelm you. Start with phase one and only look at the screen you're working on."

### "It Makes Sense To" Instead of "You Should"

When connecting two steps where the second follows naturally from the first, this phrase feels collaborative rather than instructive.

- "It makes sense to learn the traditional structure before jumping into Next.js."
- "At this point it makes sense to check your logs."
- "It makes sense to cover this here in my opinion."

## Part 8: What to Cut Entirely

These have no place in the Brad voice, no exceptions.

**The banned expression patterns (from the base writing rules):**
- "In a world where..."
- "Most people do X. The few who win do Y."
- "Stop doing X. Start doing Y."
- "It's not about X. It's about Y."
- "If you're not doing X, you're already behind."
- "Here's the truth..."
- "You don't need more X. You need Y."
- "It's never been easier / harder to..."

**The banned words (a short hit list of the worst offenders):**
- delve, realm, harness, tapestry, paradigm
- groundbreaking, revolutionary, game-changer
- leverage, synergy, holistic, seamless
- empower, unleash, transformative

**Overused "I mean" and "I would say"**

Both are Brad patterns but both have a hard limit. "I mean" belongs once per section, only when genuinely rephrasing something. "I would say" belongs before recommendations, not before every single sentence. The READMEs in the Folio and Stackhouse projects showed what happens when these get applied at 100% saturation. The voice curdled into something that sounded unsure of itself.

**Context mismatch**

Brad's full conversational register belongs in tutorial scripts and YouTube-style walkthroughs. READMEs and technical documentation can carry his warmth, his honesty, his specific tool naming, and his admission of rough edges, but at maybe 30% saturation. A README that opens with "All right, so Folio is basically a tool..." works. A README where every paragraph has "I mean" and "I would say" and "you know" sounds like a vlog, not documentation.

---

## The One-Line Summary

Write like someone who has been building things for years, genuinely wants to help you, and is talking to you directly, not performing for an audience.
